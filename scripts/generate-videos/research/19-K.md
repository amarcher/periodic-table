## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up slow-motion of a soft, silvery chunk of potassium metal being dropped into a glass dish of water. The instant it touches the surface, it ignites spontaneously, skittering and spinning wildly across the water with a brilliant lilac-violet flame. Hydrogen gas hisses and bubbles furiously around the metal as it dances, throwing off violet sparks. The water churns and splashes from the violent exothermic reaction. The potassium shrinks rapidly as it's consumed, the violet flame reflecting off the turbulent water surface. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Potassium's reaction with water is one of the most dramatic alkali metal demonstrations in chemistry — more violent than sodium, with the distinctive lilac-violet flame color that sets it apart. The self-ignition on contact and the wild spinning motion across the water surface make for an unforgettable visual. This is a classic chemistry demonstration that never fails to captivate.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Cutting potassium with a knife (showing soft metallic interior):** Visually interesting but static and less dramatic than the water reaction
- **Potassium flame test (violet flame on a wire loop):** Too small and subtle compared to the full water reaction spectacle
- **Potassium oxidizing in air (silvery to white):** The tarnishing process is too slow and visually underwhelming

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** Very light metal (0.86 g/cm³) — floats on water, which is why it skitters across the surface while reacting
- **Reactivity:** Extremely reactive — ignites spontaneously on contact with water, must be stored under mineral oil
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Low melting point for a metal (63.5°C / 146°F)
- **Appearance:** Freshly cut surface is bright silvery, but oxidizes to flaky white within seconds in air
- **Other:** So soft it can be cut with a butter knife; seventh most abundant element in Earth's crust

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Potassium is so soft you can slice it with a butter knife, and the fresh cut surface is shiny like a mirror — but it turns dull white in just seconds!
2. Your body needs potassium to make your muscles work — bananas are famous for it, but avocados and potatoes actually have even more.
3. Potassium burns with a violet-purple flame, which is why purple fireworks often contain potassium compounds.
