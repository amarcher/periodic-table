# Antimony (Sb) — Element 51

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** A crystalline antimony chunk shattering like glass when struck, revealing its flaky, brittle crystalline interior

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a beautiful chunk of antimony metal with a lustrous, silvery-blue surface and distinctive star-shaped crystalline patterns. A small hammer strikes the metal and it shatters explosively like glass — fragments flying outward, revealing glittering, flaky crystalline layers inside. The shattered pieces scatter across the dark surface, each fragment showing the characteristic layered, plate-like crystal structure that makes antimony more brittle than glass. The broken surfaces catch the light with a silvery-blue metallic sheen. The brittle destruction of what appears to be a solid metal is shocking and counterintuitive — metals are not supposed to shatter like porcelain. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Antimony is one of the most strikingly brittle "metals" — described historically as "more brittle than glass." When struck, it shatters into beautiful flaky, crystalline fragments rather than bending or denting like a normal metal. The visual of a metallic-looking material exploding like glass is deeply counterintuitive and surprising. Plus, antimony crystals are genuinely beautiful with their star-shaped growth patterns.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Explosive antimony allotrope:** Visually dramatic (explodes when scratched) but produces toxic fumes — not kid-appropriate
- **Antimony reacting with chlorine:** Burns spontaneously but produces toxic SbCl3 fumes
- **Antimony crystals growing:** Beautiful star patterns but slow and less dramatic than the shatter

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 6.697 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Stable in air at room temperature; burns in chlorine and fluorine; has an explosive allotrope
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 630.6C, boils at 1587C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-blue metallic luster with beautiful star-shaped crystal growth patterns; extremely brittle and flaky
- **Other:** Has an explosive allotrope that detonates when scratched; classified as a metalloid

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Antimony looks like a metal but shatters like glass when you hit it — it is more brittle than a ceramic plate
2. There is a special form of antimony that is so unstable it literally explodes when you scratch it with a pin
3. The chemical symbol "Sb" comes from "stibium," the Latin word for antimony — ancient Egyptians used antimony sulfide as black eye makeup (kohl) thousands of years ago
