# Gallium (Ga) — Element 31

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a shiny, silvery chunk of solid gallium metal resting in the palm of a hand. The warmth of the skin begins to melt the metal — its edges soften and become glossy, liquid metal slowly pooling and spreading like mercury. The solid gradually collapses into a brilliant, mirror-like liquid puddle that reflects everything around it. The transformation from rigid solid to flowing liquid happens in real time, the gallium slumping and liquefying as body heat alone is enough to trigger the phase change. The liquid gallium shimmers with a bright metallic luster, pooling in the creases of the palm. Droplets of liquid metal catch the light like tiny mirrors. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Gallium melting in a human hand is one of the most mind-blowing demonstrations in all of chemistry — a real metal that melts from body heat alone. Its melting point of just 29.76°C (85.6°F) is below human body temperature, making this phase change feel almost magical. The visual of a rigid, shiny metallic solid slowly collapsing into a pool of liquid mirror is captivating and immediately understandable to kids. This is gallium's signature party trick and the reason the element is famous.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 5.91 g/cm³ — moderately dense; liquid gallium is denser than solid gallium (like water/ice)
- **Reactivity:** Low reactivity; safe to handle with bare hands (though it stains skin temporarily)
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Astonishingly low melting point of 29.76°C (85.6°F); but boiling point is very high at 2,204°C — one of the largest liquid ranges of any element
- **Appearance:** Silvery-blue metal; brilliant mirror-like surface in liquid form
- **Other:** Expands when it freezes (like water); can destroy aluminum structures by infiltrating its crystal lattice

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Gallium melts in your hand! Its melting point is just below body temperature, so holding a chunk of gallium will slowly turn it from a solid metal into a pool of liquid mirror.
2. Gallium has the largest liquid range of almost any element — it melts at just 30°C but doesn't boil until 2,204°C, meaning it stays liquid across an enormous temperature range.
3. A tiny drop of liquid gallium placed on an aluminum can will slowly eat through it — the gallium sneaks between the aluminum atoms and makes the whole structure crumble like a cookie!
