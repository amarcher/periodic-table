# Oganesson (Og) — Element 118

**Confidence:** Medium

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Close-up of a single luminous atom against pure black space. The atom pulses with inner light. Unlike normal atoms with neat orbital rings, this one has a diffuse, shimmering cloud of electrons that blur together like fog — no distinct shells, just a smooth, glowing haze. The camera slowly orbits the atom. Text fades in: "Element 118 — Oganesson." The electron cloud ripples and shifts, showing it is easily disturbed. Then the atom begins to glow brighter, destabilizes, and bursts apart into a shower of smaller glowing particles (alpha decay), each flying away and themselves breaking apart in a cascade. The whole sequence from stable atom to complete decay takes 5 seconds — matching oganesson's real half-life. Final frame: darkness, with a faint afterglow where the atom was. Mood: awe, fragility, the edge of existence. Color palette: deep blue-violet core, white-gold electron haze, warm orange decay particles.

## Why This Concept
Oganesson is the single most scientifically extraordinary element on the entire periodic table. It is formally a noble gas (group 18, alongside helium, neon, and argon), but it breaks every rule noble gases follow: it is predicted to be a solid at room temperature, it may be a semiconductor, and its electrons do not form distinct shells — instead they smear out into a near-uniform "Fermi gas" cloud. This is the only atom in the periodic table predicted to have this property. A visualization showing this bizarre electron structure would be genuinely educational and visually stunning, even though it must be conceptual rather than photographic.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (294Og) lasts about 0.69 milliseconds — less than one-thousandth of a second. Only five or six atoms have ever been detected.
- **How it was made:** First synthesized in 2002 at JINR Dubna, Russia, by Yuri Oganessian's team. They bombarded californium-249 with calcium-48 ions. Officially recognized in 2015 and named in 2016.
- **Predicted properties:** Predicted to be a SOLID noble gas — melting point around 325 K (52 degrees C / 125 degrees F). May be a semiconductor with a band gap of about 1.5 eV. Electrons form a smeared-out "Fermi gas" instead of distinct shells — unique among all known elements. Strong relativistic effects (spin-orbit coupling shifts the melting point by about 100 K). More chemically reactive than any other noble gas.
- **Other:** Named after Yuri Oganessian (born 1933), making him the only living person with an element named after them — only the second time this has ever happened (after seaborgium/Glenn Seaborg). Oganesson is the heaviest element ever created and the last element in period 7 of the periodic table.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Oganesson is supposed to be a noble gas — in the same family as helium in balloons and neon in signs. But scientists predict it would be a SOLID at room temperature, not a gas at all! It completely breaks the rules of its own family.
2. It is named after Yuri Oganessian, a scientist born in 1933 who is STILL ALIVE — making him the only living person with an element named after them. Imagine having your name on the periodic table while you are still around to see it!
3. Oganesson's electrons are so weird that they do not form normal shells like every other atom. Instead, they smear out into a fuzzy cloud called a "Fermi gas." It is the only element in the entire periodic table predicted to do this — it is like an atom that forgot how to be an atom.
