# Protactinium (Pa) — Element 91

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** No
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** A bright silvery metallic sample slowly developing a white oxide coating in air

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small, bright silvery-gray metallic sample of protactinium resting on a dark surface. The metal has a lustrous, almost mirror-like sheen. Slowly, a faint whitish film begins to creep across the surface from the edges inward as the metal reacts with air, forming a delicate protactinium oxide coating. The reflective surface gradually becomes clouded and matte, the transformation visible as a subtle wave of white overtaking the bright silver. Faint wisps of oxidation bloom across the surface like frost forming on a window. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Protactinium is extremely rare and expensive, with very few visual demonstrations available. The most honest visual representation is the metal itself — a bright, silvery surface slowly tarnishing. While not as dramatic as other actinides, this oxidation process is the most scientifically accurate thing to show.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Abstract radioactive decay visualization:** Could be done but feels generic and dishonest about what protactinium actually looks like
- **Protactinium in solution (showing oxidation states):** The chemistry is real but not visually dramatic enough for an 8-second video

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 15.37 g/cm3 — very dense, heavier than lead
- **Reactivity:** Readily reacts with oxygen, water vapor, and inorganic acids; forms a white oxide coating in air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at approximately 1568 degrees C
- **Appearance:** Bright, silvery-gray metallic luster when freshly cut; tarnishes in air
- **Other:** One of the rarest and most expensive naturally occurring elements; named "proto-actinium" because it decays into actinium

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Protactinium is one of the rarest elements on Earth — there are only a few hundred grams scattered throughout the entire planet's crust at any given time.
2. Its name literally means "parent of actinium" because when protactinium decays radioactively, it transforms into actinium — like a chemical family tree!
3. Protactinium is so rare and dangerous that it has almost no practical uses — making it one of the most mysterious elements on the periodic table.
