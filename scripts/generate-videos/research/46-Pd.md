As hydrogen gas is introduced, the silvery metal begins to visibly absorb the invisible gas like a metallic sponge — the foil subtly swells, expands, and warps as it soaks up hundreds of times its own volume of hydrogen into its crystal lattice. The surface shimmers and shifts as the internal structure transforms. Then, as the hydrogen is released, the foil slowly contracts back, and wisps of vapor form on its surface as released hydrogen meets oxygen in the air, creating tiny patches of condensation and faint warmth. ## Why This Concept
Palladium can absorb up to 900 times its own volume of hydrogen gas — like a metallic sponge for an invisible gas. This is one of the most remarkable properties of any element. The visible swelling and warping of the metal as it absorbs hydrogen is scientifically fascinating, and the concept of a "breathing" metal is captivating for kids.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 12.02 g/cm3 — dense platinum group metal
- **Reactivity:** Can absorb 900x its volume in hydrogen gas; resistant to corrosion in air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1555C, boils at 2963C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal similar to platinum
- **Other:** Expands by 3.7% when fully loaded with hydrogen; lattice structure visibly changes

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Palladium can soak up 900 times its own volume of hydrogen gas — like a metal sponge for an invisible gas that you can squeeze back out later
2. When palladium absorbs hydrogen, it actually grows bigger — expanding nearly 4% as atoms squeeze into the spaces between palladium atoms
3. Nearly every car on the road has palladium in its catalytic converter, where it helps turn toxic exhaust fumes into harmless gases
