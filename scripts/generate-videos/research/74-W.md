# Tungsten (W) — Element 74

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** A tungsten filament heating up from dark to blazing white-hot, glowing with incredible intensity without melting

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a coiled tungsten filament inside a clear glass bulb. Electric current slowly increases — the filament shifts from invisible to deep red, then bright orange, then searing yellow, and finally an almost unbearable brilliant white-hot glow that illuminates everything around it. The thin wire trembles slightly from the heat, radiating intense white light that flares and blooms in the lens. The coils glow so hot they seem to pulse with energy, yet the metal holds perfectly solid, refusing to melt at temperatures that would vaporize nearly any other element. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Tungsten has the highest melting point of any element at 3,422°C, and seeing it glow white-hot while remaining solid is a visceral demonstration of this record-breaking property. The color progression from dark to red to white is inherently dramatic and beautiful, and the concept of "so hot it glows but refuses to melt" is instantly understandable to kids.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Tungsten cube density demonstration**: Impressive weight but not visually dynamic enough for video
- **Tungsten carbide shattering on impact**: Shows brittleness of the compound, not the pure element
- **Welding with tungsten electrode (TIG)**: Too industrial, not focused on the element itself

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 19.3 g/cm3 — about 2.5x denser than steel, similar to gold
- **Reactivity:** Highly resistant to corrosion, very stable at room temperature
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Highest melting point of ALL elements at 3,422°C (6,192°F); boiling point 5,555°C
- **Appearance:** Steel-gray to silvery-white, very hard metal
- **Other:** Chemical symbol W comes from its German name "Wolfram" — the English name "Tungsten" comes from Swedish for "heavy stone"

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Tungsten has the highest melting point of any element on Earth — you would need a temperature of 3,422°C to melt it, hot enough to melt through almost anything else
2. Every old-fashioned light bulb used a tiny tungsten wire that glowed white-hot without melting — that is why your great-grandparents had light!
3. A tungsten cube the size of a baseball would weigh about as much as a bowling ball
