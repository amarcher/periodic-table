## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of the Czochralski crystal-growing process: a perfectly cylindrical ingot of pure silicon crystal is being slowly pulled upward from a crucible of molten silicon glowing bright orange-white. The solid crystal emerges with a dark blue-grey metallic luster, mirror-smooth and flawless, rotating slowly as it rises. Below it, the molten silicon surface glows intensely like liquid fire, and at the interface where solid meets liquid, a brilliant ring of light marks the crystallization front. The contrast between the glowing orange melt and the cool dark metallic crystal above is stunning. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
The Czochralski process is how nearly all computer chips begin — a single perfect crystal pulled from glowing molten silicon. The visual contrast between the brilliant orange melt and the cool blue-grey crystal is dramatic, and the slow rotation creates natural motion. It connects to every phone, computer, and game console kids use daily, making it deeply relatable.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 2.33 g/cm3 — lighter than aluminum
- **Reactivity:** Relatively inert at room temperature due to its oxide layer; becomes reactive at high temperatures
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1,414 C, boils at 3,265 C
- **Appearance:** Hard, brittle crystalline solid with a blue-grey metallic luster; in thin layers it is partially transparent to infrared light
- **Other:** Second most abundant element in Earth's crust (27.7% by weight), after oxygen

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Every phone, tablet, computer, and game console you have ever used runs on a tiny chip made from a crystal of silicon — that is why the tech capital of the world is called "Silicon Valley"
2. Silicon is the second most common element in the Earth's crust — almost every rock and grain of sand contains it
3. Pure silicon crystals are grown as single perfect cylinders that can weigh over 200 kilograms, then sliced into wafers thinner than a fingernail to make computer chips
