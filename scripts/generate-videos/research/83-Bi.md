## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of molten bismuth in a steel crucible, glowing red-hot. As the surface cools, a thin silver skin forms and is carefully pulled aside to reveal stunning geometric hopper crystals growing beneath — perfect staircase spirals and cubic formations emerging from the liquid metal. The crystals are lifted out, and as they cool in air, a mesmerizing rainbow oxide layer blooms across their surfaces in real-time: gold shifting to magenta, then electric blue, then deep purple and iridescent green, the colors rippling across the crystalline steps like an oil slick made of metal. The final crystal is a dazzling geometric rainbow sculpture. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Bismuth hopper crystals are among the most visually stunning objects in all of chemistry — perfect geometric staircases covered in iridescent rainbow colors from thin-film interference of the oxide layer. The combination of geometric perfection and rainbow iridescence is unlike anything else in the periodic table. This is an absolute showstopper for kids and one of the most photogenic elements in existence.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 9.78 g/cm3 — quite dense but lighter than lead
- **Reactivity:** Relatively stable; forms a beautiful iridescent oxide layer when exposed to air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at just 271°C — low enough to melt on a kitchen stove; boils at 1,564°C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-pink metal in pure form; hopper crystals display spectacular rainbow iridescence
- **Other:** Technically radioactive, but with a half-life of 1.9 × 10^19 years — over a billion times the age of the universe, so effectively stable

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. When melted and slowly cooled, bismuth forms incredible rainbow staircase crystals that look like they belong in a science fiction movie — you can even grow them at home!
2. Bismuth is technically radioactive, but its half-life is over a billion times longer than the entire age of the universe — so it will basically never decay
3. The pink stuff in Pepto-Bismol contains bismuth — so this rainbow crystal element might have already been in your stomach!
