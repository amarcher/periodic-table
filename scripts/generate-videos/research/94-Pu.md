# Plutonium (Pu) — Element 94

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** A plutonium-238 pellet glowing red-hot from its own radioactive decay heat, like a smoldering ember

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small, dark, ceramic-like plutonium oxide pellet resting in a graphite container. In dim lighting, the pellet radiates a deep, smoldering red-orange glow from within — heated entirely by its own radioactive decay with no external flame or power source. The glow pulses subtly, like a living ember, casting warm orange-red light onto the surrounding surfaces. Heat shimmer ripples the air above the pellet. The incandescent glow shifts between deep cherry red and bright orange across the pellet's textured surface, an eternal fire powered by atomic decay. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
The image of a plutonium-238 pellet glowing red-hot purely from its own radioactivity is one of the most iconic visuals in nuclear science. NASA has published famous photographs of this exact phenomenon. The idea that a material generates so much heat from invisible atomic decay that it literally glows like a hot coal is mind-blowing and immediately captivating — especially knowing this same glow powers spacecraft exploring the outer solar system.

- **Water boiling from plutonium's decay heat:** Dramatic concept but harder to attribute visually to the element itself

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 19.8 g/cm3 — extremely dense, nearly as heavy as gold
- **Reactivity:** Tarnishes in air from silvery to yellow to olive green to black; pyrophoric when finely divided
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 640 degrees C — unusually low for such a dense metal
- **Appearance:** Freshly prepared metal is bright silvery; a Pu-238 pellet glows red-hot from decay heat alone
- **Other:** Has six allotropic forms — more than any other element; a piece is warm to the touch from radioactive decay; large pieces can boil water

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Plutonium is so radioactive that a chunk of it feels warm to the touch — and a big enough piece glows red-hot like a coal, heated entirely by atoms breaking apart inside it!
2. NASA uses plutonium-238 to power spacecraft that travel too far from the Sun for solar panels to work — the Mars rovers Curiosity and Perseverance both run on plutonium batteries!
3. Plutonium has more crystal structures than any other element — six different ones — making it a shape-shifter of the atomic world.
