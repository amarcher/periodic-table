# Zinc (Zn) — Element 30

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Zinc metal reacting vigorously with hydrochloric acid, producing a furious stream of hydrogen bubbles

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of granulated zinc metal pieces being dropped into a glass beaker of hydrochloric acid. The instant the silvery-gray zinc hits the liquid, a violent eruption of tiny hydrogen gas bubbles begins, fizzing and roaring furiously from the metal's surface. The bubbling is intense and relentless — a churning mass of bubbles streaming upward through the clear liquid like a miniature underwater volcano. The zinc pieces visibly dissolve and shrink as they react, their surfaces alive with effervescence. Steam and gas rise from the roiling surface. The bubbles catch the light as they rise, creating a shimmering curtain of ascending spheres. The reaction accelerates as heat builds, the solution churning ever more vigorously. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Zinc's reaction with hydrochloric acid is a classic chemistry demonstration — vigorous, visually dramatic, and safe. The furious bubbling is immediately captivating and the reaction is fast enough to sustain visual interest throughout 8 seconds. The visible dissolution of the metal and the intense stream of hydrogen gas bubbles create a dynamic, action-filled visual. This is one of the most commonly demonstrated acid-metal reactions in chemistry education.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Zinc burning with a bluish-green flame:** Possible but less dramatic and distinctive than the acid reaction
- **Galvanized steel (zinc coating):** Industrial process, not visually compelling
- **Zinc crystals forming during electroplating:** Interesting but slow and subtle

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 7.13 g/cm³ — moderately dense
- **Reactivity:** Reacts vigorously with acids; corrodes slowly in moist air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 419.5°C — relatively low for a metal
- **Appearance:** Silvery-blue-gray metal; freshly cast zinc has a bright bluish-silver surface
- **Other:** Fourth most common metal in use today; essential trace element in the human body

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Zinc is the ultimate bodyguard metal — when you coat iron or steel with zinc (called "galvanizing"), the zinc sacrifices itself to corrosion so the iron doesn't rust!
2. Your body contains about 2 grams of zinc, and you need it to taste and smell things — people who don't get enough zinc literally can't taste their food properly.
3. The U.S. penny has been mostly zinc since 1982 — it's 97.5% zinc with just a thin copper coating on the outside!
