## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a mound of vivid brick-red amorphous selenium powder in a glass dish being slowly heated. The brilliant red powder begins to darken, shifting from bright scarlet-red to deep crimson, then to a rich blood-red as the temperature climbs. As it softens at 50°C, the powder melts into a dark red viscous liquid. With continued heating the liquid darkens further, transforming through deep ruby to black as it reaches 180°C and converts to the gray metallic form. The final gray selenium has a lustrous metallic sheen, completely transformed from the original vivid red powder. The dramatic color journey from bright red through every shade to metallic gray unfolds in a mesmerizing continuous transformation. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Selenium's multiple allotropes create one of the most visually dramatic color transformations of any element. Starting from a vivid brick-red powder (amorphous selenium), heating causes a continuous visible transformation through deep red, black, and finally metallic gray. The range of colors — all from the same pure element — is stunning and immediately raises the question "how can one element look so different?" This is visually rich and scientifically educational.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 4.81 g/cm³ (gray form)
- **Reactivity:** Burns in air with a bright blue flame; its conductivity increases when exposed to light (photoconductive)
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Gray form melts at 221°C; red amorphous form softens at 50°C
- **Appearance:** Exists as brick-red powder (amorphous), red crystals (monoclinic), black glassy beads (vitreous), and gray metallic crystals (hexagonal)
- **Other:** Its electrical conductivity increases 1,000-fold when exposed to light — the basis for early photocopiers and light meters

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Selenium is the chameleon of elements — it can be bright red, deep black, or shiny metallic gray, all depending on how its atoms are arranged!
2. Selenium conducts electricity 1,000 times better when light shines on it — this weird property was used to invent the first photocopiers and the "electric eye" light sensors on elevator doors.
3. Selenium is named after Selene, the Greek goddess of the Moon, because it was found alongside tellurium (named after the Earth) — so the two elements are named after the Earth and Moon!
