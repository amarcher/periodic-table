## Why This Concept
Copernicium is one of the most scientifically fascinating superheavy elements. It sits below mercury on the periodic table and should be a metal, but relativistic effects make its outer electrons so tightly bound that it behaves more like a noble gas — specifically like radon. Computer simulations predict it is a volatile liquid that melts around 10 degrees C and boils at 67 degrees C, with a huge electronic band gap of 6.4 eV. This "identity crisis" between metal and noble gas is genuinely weird and conceptually visualizable.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (285Cn) lasts about 30 seconds. Some isotopes on the predicted "island of stability" (291Cn, 293Cn) might last decades if they could be made.
- **How it was made:** First synthesized in February 1996 at GSI Darmstadt, Germany. Researchers bombarded lead-208 with zinc-70 ions. Officially recognized in 2009.
- **Predicted properties:** A volatile "noble liquid" — melting point around 10 degrees C, boiling point around 67 degrees C. Despite being below mercury, it behaves more like radon due to relativistic stabilization of its 7s electrons. Band gap of 6.4 eV — more like a noble gas than a metal.
- **Other:** Named after Nicolaus Copernicus, who proposed the heliocentric model of the solar system. Copernicium is the last element discovered at GSI Darmstadt, completing their six-element streak (107-112).

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Copernicium sits directly below mercury on the periodic table and should be a liquid metal, but Einstein's theory of relativity changes everything — its electrons move so fast (close to the speed of light) that they make it act more like a noble gas instead.
2. Computer simulations predict copernicium would melt at about 10 degrees C (50 degrees F) — roughly the temperature of a cool spring morning. It would be liquid at room temperature, like mercury, but way more volatile.
3. Some scientists think that if we could make heavier versions of copernicium with more neutrons, they might be stable for years or even decades — they would sit on the theoretical "island of stability" in the periodic table.
