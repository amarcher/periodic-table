## Why This Concept
The most interesting thing about seaborgium is not the element itself but its namesake: Glenn T. Seaborg, who personally discovered or co-discovered 10 elements. It was controversially named after him while he was still alive — only the second time that had ever happened. But there is nothing physical to film.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (271Sg) lasts about 1.9 minutes.
- **How it was made:** Synthesized in 1974 at Lawrence Berkeley Lab by bombarding californium-249 with oxygen-18. Soviet team at Dubna also claimed synthesis the same year by bombarding lead with chromium-54. Berkeley team officially credited in 1993.
- **Predicted properties:** Behaves like tungsten (group 6 homologue). Chemistry experiments have confirmed this.
- **Other:** Named after Glenn T. Seaborg, who co-discovered 10 elements — more than any other person. The naming was controversial because IUPAC had an informal rule against naming elements after living people.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Seaborgium is named after Glenn Seaborg, a scientist who helped discover TEN elements — more than anyone else in history. He could literally write his address using only element symbols he helped discover.
2. When they tried to name it after Seaborg while he was still alive, it caused a huge argument. Some scientists said you should not name an element after a living person. Seaborg won in the end — the name became official in 1997.
3. The most stable version of seaborgium lasts less than 2 minutes. In the time it takes to brush your teeth, every atom of seaborgium you could ever make would have already disappeared.
