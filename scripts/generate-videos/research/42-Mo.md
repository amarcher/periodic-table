## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a thick molybdenum metal rod being heated in a vacuum chamber. As the temperature climbs, the rod begins to glow — first a deep cherry red, then bright orange, then an intense yellow-white as it approaches its extraordinary melting point of 2623 degrees Celsius. The rod radiates searing light like a miniature sun, heat waves shimmering around it. The surface remains perfectly solid and smooth even at white-hot temperatures where most metals would have long since melted into puddles. The brilliant incandescent glow illuminates the vacuum chamber walls with warm orange light. The rod pulses with barely contained thermal energy. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Molybdenum has the sixth highest melting point of all elements, allowing it to be heated to spectacular white-hot temperatures while remaining perfectly solid. The visual of a metal rod glowing white-hot — far beyond where steel or aluminum would have melted — dramatically communicates its extraordinary heat resistance. This extreme incandescence is visually stunning.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) as lubricant:** Technically a compound, and grey powder is not visually exciting
- **Molybdenum oxidizing in air above 760C:** The white MoO3 sublimate is interesting but looks like wisps of white smoke, less dramatic
- **Bulk molybdenum metal at room temperature:** Just a silvery-grey metal, unremarkable visually

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 10.28 g/cm3 — noticeably heavy
- **Reactivity:** Extremely stable at room temperature; rapidly oxidizes above 760C in air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 2623C — sixth highest of all elements; boils at 4639C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-grey metallic luster
- **Other:** Essential trace element for life; molybdenum enzymes are critical for nitrogen fixation in plants

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Molybdenum can stay solid at temperatures hot enough to melt through steel, aluminum, gold, and iron combined
2. Without molybdenum, plants could not convert nitrogen from the air into food — it is an essential element for all life on Earth
3. The name comes from the Greek word "molybdos" meaning lead, because people kept confusing molybdenum ore with lead ore for centuries
