## Why This Concept
Hassium has the most interesting chemistry story of any superheavy element. In 2001, scientists created just seven atoms of hassium, let them react with oxygen, and detected the resulting hassium tetroxide — proving that a superheavy element still follows the periodic table's rules. This was a triumph of the periodic table's predictive power. However, the actual experiment involves invisible quantities and specialized detectors, so it is not filmable.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (276Hs) lasts about 1.1 hours. Some isotopes last only milliseconds.
- **How it was made:** Synthesized in 1984 at GSI Darmstadt, Germany, by Peter Armbruster and Gottfried Munzenberg. They bombarded lead-208 with iron-58 ions. Earlier Soviet attempts date to 1978.
- **Predicted properties:** Behaves like osmium (group 8). This was confirmed experimentally — hassium forms a volatile tetroxide (HsO4) just like osmium forms OsO4.
- **Other:** Named after the German state of Hesse (Hassia in Latin), where GSI Darmstadt is located. The 2001 hassium tetroxide experiment is considered one of the greatest achievements in superheavy element chemistry.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. In 2001, scientists created just SEVEN atoms of hassium, mixed them with oxygen, and detected that they formed hassium tetroxide — proving that even the heaviest artificial elements still follow the rules of the periodic table.
2. Hassium is named after Hesse, a state in Germany. It is one of the few elements named after a place that is not a country, city, or lab — just a regular region.
3. Hassium's longest-lived isotope lasts about an hour, which is actually pretty impressive — that is long enough to run a chemistry experiment on it, which scientists have actually done.
