## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of fine silvery hafnium metal powder being sprinkled from a glass vial onto a ceramic dish in a laboratory. The instant the powder contacts air, it ignites spontaneously in a cascade of brilliant white-hot sparks and intense orange-white flames, each grain becoming a tiny blazing star as it falls. The pyrophoric metal flares with dazzling incandescence, sparks ricocheting off the dish and trailing bright streaks through the air like miniature fireworks. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Hafnium powder is pyrophoric — it spontaneously ignites in air, creating a spectacular shower of white-hot sparks. This is by far its most visually dramatic property and makes for a captivating, almost magical display. The contrast of the calm silvery powder suddenly erupting into brilliant fire is perfect for grabbing a kid's attention.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Silvery metal rod being machined**: Visually boring — hafnium looks like any other shiny gray metal in bulk form
- **Nuclear reactor control rods**: Important application but not visually interesting and hard to show the element itself
- **Hafnium crystal structure**: Too abstract, not dramatic enough for kids

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 13.31 g/cm3 — about 1.7x denser than steel
- **Reactivity:** Pyrophoric as a fine powder — ignites spontaneously in air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Very high melting point of 2,233°C and boiling point of 4,603°C
- **Appearance:** Lustrous silvery-gray metal that forms a protective oxide layer in air
- **Other:** Nearly identical chemically to zirconium but with opposite nuclear properties — hafnium absorbs neutrons while zirconium lets them pass through

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Hafnium powder can catch fire all by itself just from touching air — no match needed!
2. Hafnium is used in nuclear reactor control rods because it's incredibly good at catching neutrons — like a neutron sponge
3. Hafnium and zirconium are chemical twins that are almost impossible to tell apart, yet they have opposite superpowers in nuclear reactors
