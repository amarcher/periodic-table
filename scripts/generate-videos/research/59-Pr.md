# Praseodymium (Pr) — Element 59

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Praseodymium metal slowly corroding in air, developing a vivid green oxide crust that spreads like emerald rust

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Time-lapse extreme close-up of a freshly cut, bright silvery praseodymium metal surface exposed to air. Over seconds, a vivid green oxide layer begins creeping across the metallic surface like emerald frost spreading on a window. The green crust grows thicker and more textured, spalling and flaking to reveal fresh metal beneath that immediately begins greening again. The oxide layer has a rich, saturated green-yellow color — nature painting the metal in real time. Tiny flakes of green oxide curl and peel away as new layers form beneath. A metal that rusts green instead of orange. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Praseodymium's name literally means "green twin" in Greek, and its oxide is a striking vivid green — completely unlike the rusty orange of iron. This unusual green "rust" is visually distinctive and immediately sets praseodymium apart from other silvery lanthanides. A centimeter-sized sample corrodes completely in about a year, so a time-lapse captures this beautifully.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 6.77 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Corrodes in air forming a distinctive green spalling oxide layer; a 1cm sample fully corrodes in ~1 year
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 931C, boils at 3520C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal that develops a unique green oxide coating (unlike any other element)
- **Other:** Used in "didymium" glass for welding goggles and special camera filters

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Praseodymium's name means "green twin" in Greek — and it really does turn bright green when it rusts!
2. Unlike iron which rusts orange, praseodymium is the only element that rusts a vivid emerald green
3. Special praseodymium glass is used in welder's goggles to protect their eyes from the blinding light of welding torches
