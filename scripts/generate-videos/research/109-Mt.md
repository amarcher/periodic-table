## Why This Concept
Meitnerium's most compelling story is not about the element but about its namesake, Lise Meitner, who was one of the most unjustly overlooked scientists in history. She co-discovered nuclear fission with Otto Hahn, but Hahn alone received the Nobel Prize. Naming element 109 after her was a long-overdue recognition. However, the element itself has no observable properties — nothing to film.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable confirmed isotope (278Mt) lasts about 4.5-8 seconds. An unconfirmed isotope (282Mt) may last about 67 seconds.
- **How it was made:** First synthesized in August 1982 at GSI Darmstadt, Germany. Researchers bombarded bismuth-209 with iron-58 ions for about 10 days, producing a single atom of meitnerium-266.
- **Predicted properties:** Expected to behave like iridium (group 9). No experimental chemistry has been performed — it has received less chemical attention than heavier elements like copernicium and flerovium.
- **Other:** Named after Lise Meitner in 1997. She was an Austrian-Swedish physicist who, along with Otto Hahn, discovered nuclear fission in 1938.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Meitnerium is named after Lise Meitner, a physicist who helped discover nuclear fission (splitting atoms) but was unfairly left out when her lab partner Otto Hahn received the Nobel Prize. Naming this element after her was the science world's way of saying sorry.
2. To create the very first atom of meitnerium, scientists had to fire iron atoms at a bismuth target for 10 straight days before a single atom of element 109 appeared.
3. Meitnerium is one of the least-studied superheavy elements — scientists know so little about its chemistry that they are not even sure if it behaves like iridium, the element directly above it on the periodic table.
