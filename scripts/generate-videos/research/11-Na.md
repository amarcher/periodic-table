## Why This Concept
The sodium-in-water reaction is one of the most famous and dramatic chemistry demonstrations — a shiny piece of metal that dances, hisses, and bursts into yellow-orange flames the instant it touches water. It perfectly illustrates alkali metal reactivity in a visceral, unforgettable way. The bright yellow flame color is also scientifically meaningful — it's the characteristic emission line of sodium, the same light that makes street lamps glow orange.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered

- **Molten sodium as a reactor coolant:** Important industrial use but visually it just looks like a silver liquid — not distinctive

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 0.97 g/cm³ — less dense than water, so it floats on the surface while reacting violently with it
- **Reactivity:** Extremely reactive; explodes on contact with water; tarnishes within seconds when exposed to air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at just 98°C — barely above the boiling point of water
- **Appearance:** Soft, silvery-white metal that can be cut with a butter knife; freshly cut surface is shiny but dulls to gray within seconds in air
- **Other:** Sixth most abundant element on Earth; essential for nerve and muscle function in every animal on the planet

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Sodium is so soft you can slice it with a butter knife like cold cheese — but don't let that fool you, because the moment it touches water, it explodes into flames!
2. If you could extract all the salt (sodium chloride) dissolved in Earth's oceans, it would cover every continent in a layer nearly 500 feet deep — that's a 50-story building made of salt.
3. Sodium burns with a brilliant yellow-orange flame, and that's the exact same color you see in old-fashioned orange street lights — they're literally powered by glowing sodium vapor.
