# Americium (Am) — Element 95

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** An ionization smoke detector opened to reveal its americium-241 source, with alpha particles visualized as streams of invisible ions detecting wisps of smoke

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of the interior of an ionization smoke detector, revealing the small americium-241 source disc at the center of the ionization chamber. A faint silvery metallic disc sits between two metal plates. Invisible alpha particles stream outward from the source, visualized as subtle shimmering lines of ionized air filling the chamber with a delicate electric current. Then thin wisps of smoke drift into the chamber, disrupting the streams — the ionized pathways scatter and break apart as smoke particles absorb the alpha radiation. The disruption triggers the alarm circuit, a tiny LED flashing red. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Americium is the one transuranic element that virtually every person has in their home — inside smoke detectors. Showing kids exactly how the element they sleep near every night actually works is both educational and awe-inspiring. The concept of invisible radioactive particles silently guarding against fire is dramatic and relatable.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Silvery americium metal tarnishing in air:** Too similar to many other actinide metals and not distinctive
- **Americium's radioactive glow:** Americium does not produce a visually dramatic glow like actinium or curium
- **Abstract alpha particle radiation visualization:** Less compelling than showing the real-world smoke detector application kids can relate to

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 12 g/cm3 — denser than lead
- **Reactivity:** Slowly tarnishes in air from silvery-white to a dull finish
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1176 degrees C, boils at 2607 degrees C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metallic lustre when freshly prepared; slowly tarnishes in dry air
- **Other:** The most commonly encountered transuranic element — found in nearly every ionization smoke detector in the world (about 0.29 micrograms per detector)

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. You probably sleep within a few feet of americium every single night — it is the radioactive element inside most household smoke detectors that helps keep you safe from fires!
2. The tiny amount of americium in a smoke detector (less than a millionth of a gram) shoots out invisible alpha particles that create an electric current in the air — when smoke breaks that current, the alarm screams!
3. Americium was first made in 1944 during the Manhattan Project and named after the Americas — just as europium was named after Europe.
