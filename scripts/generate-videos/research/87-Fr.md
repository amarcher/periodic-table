## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
N/A — Francium cannot be filmed because no visible quantity has ever been assembled. Its most stable isotope, francium-223, has a half-life of only 22 minutes, and the largest sample ever produced contained only about 300,000 atoms — far too few to see. While it is theoretically the most reactive metal (its reaction with water would be even more explosive than cesium), this has never been observed and likely never will be.

## Why This Concept
Francium is fascinating in theory — as the most reactive metal, its reaction with water would theoretically be spectacularly explosive, far surpassing cesium. However, it simply cannot be observed. The largest amount ever collected was a cluster of about 300,000 atoms, invisible to the naked eye, and it decayed within minutes. Any video would require pure CGI speculation, which would not be scientifically honest.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** Estimated ~2.48 g/cm3 (never directly measured)
- **Reactivity:** Theoretically the most reactive metal — would react explosively with water, more violently than cesium
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Estimated melting point ~27°C (near room temperature!), boiling point ~677°C
- **Appearance:** Never observed in visible quantities — predicted to be a silvery metallic alkali metal
- **Other:** Most unstable naturally occurring element; most stable isotope (Fr-223) has a half-life of only 22 minutes

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Francium is theoretically the most reactive metal in existence — if you could get enough of it and drop it in water, the explosion would make cesium look gentle
2. The largest amount of francium ever gathered in one place was about 300,000 atoms — so few that they were completely invisible, even under a microscope
3. Francium's most stable form lasts only 22 minutes before it radioactively decays — blink and it is gone
