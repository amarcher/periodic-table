# Scandium (Sc) — Element 21

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** No
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Scandium metal slowly tarnishing from silvery-white to yellowish as oxide forms

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a polished piece of scandium metal with a bright, silvery-white metallic surface reflecting light. Over time the surface gradually develops a warm yellowish-pink tint as a thin layer of scandium oxide forms on exposure to air. The camera slowly rotates around the lustrous metal piece, catching light reflections that shift from bright silver to subtle gold-pink hues across the oxidizing surface. Tiny iridescent interference colors shimmer where the oxide layer is thinnest. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Scandium is one of the rarer and less visually dramatic elements. It doesn't have spectacular reactions with water or produce colorful flames. Its most visually interesting property is the subtle color change from silvery-white to yellowish-pink as it oxidizes, but this is a slow process and not particularly exciting for an 8-second video. The element is simply too unreactive and visually plain for a compelling video.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Scandium dissolving in dilute acid:** Slow dissolution with modest bubbling — not visually distinctive
- **Polished scandium reflecting light:** Beautiful but static, no action or drama
- **Scandium oxide powder:** White powder is visually boring

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 2.99 g/cm³ — relatively light for a transition metal
- **Reactivity:** Moderately reactive, slowly dissolves in dilute acids
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** High melting point of 1,541°C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal that develops a yellowish or pinkish cast when oxidized
- **Other:** The first pound of pure scandium wasn't produced until 1960, over 80 years after its discovery

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Scandium was predicted to exist before it was actually found — a scientist named Mendeleev left a blank spot for it in his periodic table and guessed its properties almost perfectly!
2. Scandium is added to aluminum to make super-strong, lightweight alloys used in fighter jets and high-end baseball bats.
3. Despite being classified as a "rare earth" element, scandium is actually more common in Earth's crust than lead or mercury — it's just spread out thinly everywhere instead of being concentrated in one place.
