# Iodine (I) — Element 53

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of dark, lustrous, almost black iodine crystals with a faint purple-metallic sheen sitting in a round glass flask. Gentle heat is applied from below and the crystals begin to sublimate — transforming directly from solid to gas without ever becoming liquid. Rich, deep purple-violet vapor rises from the crystals and fills the flask like smoke from a wizard's cauldron. The heavy purple gas swirls and billows in thick, luxurious clouds, the vivid violet color intensifying as more vapor accumulates. At the top of the flask, a cold glass surface causes the vapor to re-crystallize into beautiful, glittering purple-black crystals that grow and branch in intricate patterns. The entire flask glows with an ethereal violet light. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Iodine is the only element that sublimes into a vividly colored gas at modest temperatures. The deep purple-violet vapor is immediately visually stunning — rich, saturated, and utterly unlike any other element's vapor. The fact that it skips the liquid phase entirely (solid straight to gas) adds scientific fascination. The re-crystallization on a cold surface adds a magical secondary visual. This is one of the most photogenic demonstrations in all of chemistry.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 4.933 g/cm3 (solid)
- **Reactivity:** Sublimes at room temperature (slowly); reacts with many metals and organic compounds
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 113.7C, boils at 184.3C — very low; famous for sublimation
- **Appearance:** Dark, lustrous purple-black crystals with a metallic sheen; produces vivid purple vapor
- **Other:** One of the few elements that sublimes directly from solid to gas at atmospheric pressure

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Iodine is the only element that turns into a vivid purple gas — it skips being a liquid entirely and goes straight from dark crystals to purple smoke
2. The name iodine comes from the Greek word "iodes" meaning violet, because of its stunning purple vapor
3. Your body needs tiny amounts of iodine to make thyroid hormones — that is why table salt has iodine added to it
