## Why This Concept
Thallium's fame comes entirely from its toxicity — it was historically used as rat poison and in notorious poisoning cases. Its physical properties (soft, tarnishes quickly, looks like lead) are not visually distinctive. Showing toxicity effects would be inappropriate for kids, and simply showing the metal itself would be boring.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 11.85 g/cm3 — quite dense, heavier than lead
- **Reactivity:** Reacts with water and tarnishes rapidly in air, forming a bluish-gray oxide layer
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 304°C, boils at 1,473°C — relatively low for a metal
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white when fresh, quickly tarnishes to bluish-gray resembling lead
- **Other:** So soft it can be cut with a kitchen knife; extremely toxic — was once called "the poisoner's poison" because it is tasteless and odorless

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Thallium is so soft you can cut it with a butter knife, just like cheese
2. It was named from the Greek word "thallos" meaning "green twig" because it produces a brilliant green line when viewed through a spectroscope
3. Thallium is one of the most toxic elements — Agatha Christie even used it as the murder weapon in one of her mystery novels
