# Gadolinium (Gd) — Element 64

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** A gadolinium sample clinging to a magnet, then being gently heated until it suddenly loses magnetism and drops away

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small, shiny silver gadolinium metal sample hanging from a powerful permanent magnet in a dark laboratory. The metal clings firmly to the magnet, clearly ferromagnetic. A gentle stream of warm air from a heat gun begins warming the gadolinium. At around 20 degrees Celsius — barely above room temperature — the gadolinium suddenly and dramatically releases from the magnet and falls away, tumbling downward. The invisible magnetic force that held it simply vanishes as thermal energy disrupts the magnetic ordering. The metal is cooled and brought back — it snaps to the magnet again. Heated once more, it drops. A metal that switches its magnetism on and off with just a few degrees of temperature change. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Gadolinium is the only element that is ferromagnetic at room temperature besides iron, cobalt, and nickel — but its Curie point (where it loses magnetism) is only ~20C, right at room temperature. This means you can watch magnetism literally switch off with just gentle warming. The sudden "drop" when the metal loses its magnetic grip is a dramatic visual moment that makes an invisible force suddenly visible. Kids can immediately grasp the concept of temperature controlling magnetism.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 7.90 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Slowly tarnishes in moist air; reacts slowly with water
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1313C, boils at 3273C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal with a metallic luster
- **Other:** Ferromagnetic just below room temperature — Curie point at ~20C (68F), the lowest of any element

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Gadolinium is magnetic at room temperature, but warm it up just a little and the magnetism completely vanishes — you can turn its magnetism on and off with a hair dryer!
2. Doctors inject gadolinium into patients before MRI scans because it makes organs and blood vessels glow brightly on the scan images
3. Gadolinium has the highest neutron absorption of any element, making it useful in nuclear reactor control rods as an emergency shutdown material
