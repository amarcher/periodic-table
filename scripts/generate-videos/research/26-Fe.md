# Iron (Fe) — Element 26

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** Steel wool ignited in pure oxygen, burning with a cascade of brilliant orange-white sparks raining downward

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a bundle of fine steel wool suspended in a glass jar filled with pure oxygen. A single spark ignites it and the iron erupts into a dazzling cascade of combustion — brilliant orange and white sparks showering downward like a miniature fireworks display. The steel wool glows intensely, shifting from deep red to blinding orange-white as the iron burns furiously in the oxygen-rich atmosphere. Molten droplets of iron oxide rain down in bright streaks, each one a tiny meteor trailing light. The combustion races through the steel wool, consuming it in a spectacular chain reaction of heat and light, the sparks reflecting off the glass walls. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Steel wool burning in pure oxygen is one of the most visually spectacular chemistry demonstrations — the iron reacts so vigorously that it produces a cascade of molten sparks that look like a contained fireworks show. The dramatic difference between iron's behavior in normal air (gentle glowing) versus pure oxygen (explosive combustion) perfectly illustrates how reactive iron truly is. This is far more dramatic than showing rust formation or a simple iron bar.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Thermite reaction (molten iron pouring):** Extremely dramatic but involves aluminum as well, making it less about pure iron; also dangerously bright UV light
- **Iron rusting (time-lapse):** Scientifically important but visually slow and not dramatic enough for 8 seconds
- **Molten iron being poured:** Dramatic but difficult to convey the scale in close-up

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 7.87 g/cm³ — a heavy, dense metal
- **Reactivity:** Rusts readily in moist air; burns spectacularly in pure oxygen
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1,538°C — requires extreme heat
- **Appearance:** Lustrous silvery-gray metal that develops a reddish-brown rust coating over time
- **Other:** The most used metal on Earth; Earth's core is mostly iron; it's ferromagnetic

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Earth's core is a giant ball of iron the size of the Moon — and it's so hot there (5,400°C) that it glows brighter than the surface of the Sun!
2. Your blood is red because of iron — the iron atoms in your hemoglobin grab onto oxygen and carry it to every cell in your body.
3. If you burn steel wool (thin iron threads) in a jar of pure oxygen, it erupts in a shower of brilliant sparks like a tiny fireworks show — the iron is literally on fire!
