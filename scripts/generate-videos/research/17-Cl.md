# Chlorine (Cl) — Element 17

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** Dense greenish-yellow chlorine gas swirling inside a glass flask, then steel wool lowered into it bursting into a glowing reaction with billowing reddish-brown smoke

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a large round glass flask filled with dense, swirling greenish-yellow chlorine gas — the gas is thick enough to see distinct layers and currents of vivid yellow-green color. A tuft of fine steel wool is lowered slowly into the flask on a wire. The moment it enters the chlorine, the steel wool begins to glow bright orange-red and erupts into a shower of brilliant orange sparks, burning vigorously without any flame. Voluminous clouds of reddish-brown iron chloride smoke billow and swirl inside the flask, mixing dramatically with the yellow-green chlorine gas, creating turbulent swirling patterns of contrasting colors — green-yellow and rusty brown. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Chlorine is one of the very few gases with a visible, vivid color — its dense greenish-yellow appearance is striking on its own. Combining that with the steel wool reaction gives both a beautiful static visual (the colored gas) and explosive action (the glowing metal and billowing smoke). The color contrast between the yellow-green gas and reddish-brown smoke is visually stunning and unique.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Chlorine gas alone swirling in a flask**: Beautiful color but lacks action — too passive for 8 seconds
- **Sodium burning in chlorine (making table salt)**: Dramatic but the yellow sodium flame overwhelms the green chlorine color, losing the unique visual
- **Chlorine dissolving in water**: Produces a pale yellow solution but is visually mundane

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 3.21 g/L as a gas — about 2.5 times heavier than air, so it sinks and pools
- **Reactivity:** Extremely reactive — attacks most metals, many organic compounds, and was historically used as a chemical weapon
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at -101 C, boils at -34 C (a gas at room temperature)
- **Appearance:** Dense greenish-yellow gas with a sharp, suffocating odor; the name "chlorine" comes from the Greek word for green
- **Other:** Chlorine is used to purify almost all drinking water in the world and to keep swimming pools clean

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Chlorine is one of the only gases you can actually see — it forms a creepy greenish-yellow cloud that is heavier than air and sinks to the ground
2. That "pool smell" you notice at swimming pools is not actually chlorine itself — it is chlorine reacting with sweat, oils, and other stuff people bring into the water
3. When you sprinkle table salt on your food, you are eating a compound made of two dangerous elements: explosive sodium metal and toxic chlorine gas — but together they are perfectly safe and delicious
