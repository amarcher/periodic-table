## Why This Concept
Roentgenium is named after Wilhelm Rontgen, who discovered X-rays. The X-ray connection is visually evocative, but it has nothing to do with element 111's actual properties. The element itself is invisible, exists for seconds to minutes, and has never been chemically studied. There is nothing to film.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable confirmed isotope (282Rg) lasts about 130 seconds (~2 minutes). An unconfirmed isotope (286Rg) may last about 10.7 minutes.
- **How it was made:** First synthesized in December 1994 at GSI Darmstadt, Germany. Researchers bombarded bismuth-209 with nickel-64 ions.
- **Predicted properties:** Expected to behave like gold (group 11). Predicted to be an extremely dense solid metal (22-24 g/cm3). No chemical experiments have been performed.
- **Other:** Named after Wilhelm Conrad Rontgen, who discovered X-rays in 1895. The name was proposed by GSI in 2004 and accepted by IUPAC the same year.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Roentgenium is named after Wilhelm Rontgen, the physicist who discovered X-rays — the same X-rays doctors use to see your bones. He won the very first Nobel Prize in Physics in 1901.
2. Roentgenium sits directly below gold on the periodic table, so theoretically it should be gold's heavier sibling. But since only a few atoms have ever existed for about 2 minutes each, nobody knows what color it would actually be.
3. If you could somehow collect enough roentgenium to hold in your hand (you absolutely cannot), it would be one of the densest substances ever — about twice as dense as lead.
