# Neodymium (Nd) — Element 60

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** Two powerful neodymium magnets slamming together with devastating force, crushing an apple between them in slow motion

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up slow-motion of two large, shiny silver neodymium magnets being released from opposite sides with a red apple placed between them. The magnets accelerate toward each other with terrifying speed, slamming together with explosive force — the apple is instantly crushed and obliterated, juice and fragments spraying outward in a dramatic starburst pattern. The sheer magnetic force is visible in the violence of the collision. Droplets of apple juice hang suspended in the slow-motion capture as shattered fragments scatter in every direction. The world's most powerful permanent magnets demonstrating their incredible attractive force. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Neodymium magnets are the strongest permanent magnets in existence, and their power is best demonstrated through destruction. Kids are fascinated by magnets, and seeing two magnets obliterate a solid object makes the invisible force of magnetism suddenly very real and dramatic. The slow-motion apple crush is a viral-worthy visual that perfectly communicates neodymium's superpower.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 7.01 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Oxidizes in air; tarnishes to a dull oxide coating
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1024C, boils at 3074C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal with slight yellowish tinge
- **Other:** Neodymium magnets (NdFeB) are the strongest permanent magnets ever made — a small one can lift 1000x its own weight

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. A tiny neodymium magnet the size of a coin can lift over 1000 times its own weight — like a house cat lifting a school bus
2. Neodymium magnets are inside every smartphone, every electric car motor, every pair of earbuds, and every hard drive
3. Two large neodymium magnets can crush bones if body parts get caught between them — they are genuinely dangerous to handle
