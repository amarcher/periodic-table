## Why This Concept
Bromine's most visually striking property is that it is a deep reddish-brown liquid that produces thick, dramatic red-brown vapor at room temperature. Watching bromine vapor billow from a vial is immediately arresting — it looks ominous and alien, unlike any other element. It is one of only two elements that are liquid at room temperature, and the only non-metal that is, making this visual uniquely bromine's own.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 3.12 g/cm³ as a liquid — over 3 times heavier than water; vapor is 5.5 times heavier than air
- **Reactivity:** Highly reactive halogen; corrosive to skin and tissue; vapors are extremely irritating and toxic
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at -7.2°C and boils at just 58.8°C — liquid at room temperature with intense evaporation
- **Appearance:** Deep reddish-brown fuming liquid with a suffocating, acrid smell; one of only two elements that are liquid at room temperature
- **Other:** Named from the Greek "bromos" meaning "stench" — one of the few elements literally named after how bad it smells

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Bromine is one of only two elements that are liquid at room temperature (the other is mercury) — and it's the only non-metal liquid, making it truly one of a kind on the periodic table.
2. Bromine's name comes from the Greek word "bromos" meaning "stench" — scientists thought it smelled so horrible that they named the entire element after its awful odor!
3. The reddish-brown vapor that rises from bromine is so dense and heavy (over 5 times heavier than air) that it sinks and pours downward like a spooky, toxic fog — you can literally pour bromine vapor from one container to another like an invisible liquid.
