## Why This Concept
Nihonium's most compelling story is the sheer perseverance of the RIKEN team in Japan. They fired zinc ions at bismuth targets over 100 trillion times to produce a single atom. The 2004 discovery made nihonium the first element ever discovered in Asia — a point of enormous national pride for Japan. However, the element itself is invisible and lasts about 10 seconds.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (286Nh) lasts about 10 seconds.
- **How it was made:** First synthesized in July 2004 at RIKEN, Japan, by Kosuke Morita's team. They bombarded bismuth-209 with zinc-70 ions. Additional atoms were produced in 2005 and 2012. Officially recognized in December 2015.
- **Predicted properties:** Expected to behave somewhat like thallium (group 13), but relativistic effects may cause significant deviations. Very little is known experimentally.
- **Other:** First element discovered in Asia. Named "nihonium" from "Nihon" (one of the Japanese words for Japan, meaning "Land of the Rising Sun"). The discovery was a source of major national pride.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Nihonium is the first element ever discovered in Asia! Japanese scientists at the RIKEN laboratory had to smash zinc atoms into bismuth over 100 TRILLION times before they produced a single atom of element 113.
2. The name "nihonium" comes from "Nihon," which is one of the Japanese words for Japan — it literally means "Land of the Rising Sun." The element's symbol Nh appears on Japanese postage stamps.
3. The scientists ran their experiment for 80 days straight without stopping to create just one atom. In total, the discovery took from 2004 to 2012 (three separate detection events) before the world officially accepted that Japan had found a new element.
