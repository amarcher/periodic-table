# Radium (Ra) — Element 88

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** Radium mixed with phosphor paint producing an iconic eerie green glow in total darkness — powered by radioactivity alone

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a sealed glass vial in a dimly lit laboratory. Inside the vial, a small amount of radium compound is mixed with zinc sulfide phosphor. The laboratory lights slowly fade to complete darkness, and the vial reveals a haunting, steady green glow — not faint or flickering, but a confident, luminous emerald radiance that needs no batteries, no charging, no sunlight. The camera slowly pushes in on the glowing vial as wisps of green light illuminate the glass surface and cast soft green reflections on the dark surroundings. This glow will last for decades, powered by nothing but the relentless decay of radium atoms firing radiation into the phosphor. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
The green glow of radium-activated phosphor is one of the most iconic images in the history of science. It is the original "glow in the dark" — the real thing that inspired decades of science fiction and pop culture imagery of radioactive materials. The steady, self-powered green luminescence is hauntingly beautiful and immediately recognizable. For kids, the idea of a substance that glows forever without any power source is genuinely magical.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 5.5 g/cm3 — moderate density for a metal
- **Reactivity:** Highly reactive alkaline earth metal; reacts vigorously with water; tarnishes in air from white to black
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 700°C, boils at 1,737°C
- **Appearance:** Pure radium is brilliant white, freshly prepared, but tarnishes rapidly to black in air; with phosphor, produces iconic green or orange glow
- **Other:** Half-life of 1,600 years (Ra-226); discovered by Marie and Pierre Curie in 1898; one gram of radium produces about 1 calorie of heat per hour continuously

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Radium is the original "glow in the dark" element — when mixed with special paint, it produces an eerie green glow that lasts for decades, powered by nothing but its own radioactivity
2. Marie Curie discovered radium in 1898 and carried glowing vials of it in her pockets — we now know that was extremely dangerous, but at the time people thought radium was a miracle cure
3. Radium was once added to toothpaste, chocolate, and even drinking water because people believed it was healthy — it took decades to realize how dangerous radioactivity really is
