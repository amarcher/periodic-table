# Radon (Rn) — Element 86

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Radon gas condensing at low temperature and glowing with an eerie yellow-to-orange radioluminescence

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a sealed glass tube being slowly cooled in a laboratory cryogenic setup. As the temperature drops, invisible radon gas begins to condense on the inner walls of the tube. The laboratory lights dim to total darkness, and the condensed radon reveals a stunning radioluminescent glow — first a soft yellow, then intensifying to a vivid orange-red as the temperature continues to drop. The glowing liquid clings to the cold glass walls, pulsing with an otherworldly luminescence powered entirely by its own radioactive decay. The tube glows like a tiny neon sign with no electricity — lit from within by pure atomic energy. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Radon's radioluminescence is genuinely spectacular — when cooled and condensed, it produces a brilliant glow that shifts from yellow to orange-red, powered entirely by its own radioactive decay. This is one of the most visually dramatic properties of any noble gas, and the concept of a gas that glows by itself with no external energy is inherently magical and captivating for kids.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Radon detection in a home basement**: Important but not visually interesting — radon is invisible at room temperature
- **Cloud chamber showing radon decay tracks**: Educational but requires context that does not fit 8 seconds
- **Radon in a discharge tube**: Many noble gases glow in discharge tubes, not unique enough

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 9.73 kg/m3 as gas — about 8 times denser than normal air; the heaviest noble gas
- **Reactivity:** Noble gas — essentially inert, though some compounds with fluorine have been made
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at -71°C, boils at -62°C — liquid over only a 9-degree range
- **Appearance:** Colorless and odorless gas at room temperature; glows yellow to orange-red when cooled and condensed
- **Other:** Most common source of naturally occurring radiation exposure for humans — seeps up from the ground into basements

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. When cooled down enough to become a liquid, radon glows an eerie orange-red — powered entirely by its own radioactivity, no batteries or electricity needed
2. Radon is invisible and odorless, but it is the second leading cause of lung cancer — many homes have special radon detectors in their basements
3. Radon is the heaviest noble gas — if you could fill a balloon with it (please do not), it would sink to the floor because it is 8 times heavier than air
