# Thorium (Th) — Element 90

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** Thorium metal turnings igniting in air and burning with a blindingly brilliant white light

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of fine silvery thorium metal turnings piled on a dark ceramic surface. A spark ignites the edge and the metal erupts into a blazing, brilliant white incandescence — far brighter than any ordinary flame. The turnings burn with an intense, almost blinding white-hot radiance, white sparks spraying outward like miniature fireworks. The fire crawls across the pile as each shaving catches and flares, the white glow so intense it washes out the surrounding area. Wisps of white thorium dioxide smoke curl upward, illuminated from below by the searing white combustion. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Thorium's pyrophoric combustion is spectacular — it burns with one of the most brilliant white lights of any element, historically used in gas lantern mantles to light entire rooms. The visual of a metal spontaneously catching fire and burning with an almost supernatural white brilliance is both dramatic and educational, showing kids how this radioactive element once illuminated homes before electric lights.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Thorium in a gas lantern mantle glowing white:** Great context but the mantle itself is not pure thorium, just thorium-infused fabric
- **Silvery thorium metal tarnishing to black:** Too slow and visually underwhelming compared to the spectacular combustion
- **Thorium oxide's extremely high melting point demonstration:** Difficult to visualize dramatically in 8 seconds

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 11.7 g/cm3 — notably dense and heavy
- **Reactivity:** Finely divided thorium is pyrophoric — it spontaneously ignites in air; burns with a brilliant white light
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Boiling point of 4788 degrees C — the fifth highest of all elements; thorium oxide melts at 3300 degrees C, the highest of any oxide
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal that slowly tarnishes to grey, then black, when exposed to air
- **Other:** Thorium was used in gas lantern mantles for over a century to produce brilliant white light for camping lanterns

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Before electric lights were common, campers used thorium-coated lantern mantles that glowed with a brilliant white light — thorium helped light up the darkness for over 100 years!
2. Thorium could be the nuclear fuel of the future — some scientists believe thorium reactors could be safer and produce less waste than today's uranium reactors.
3. Thorium is named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder — fitting for an element that burns with the brilliance of a lightning bolt!
