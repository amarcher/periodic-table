## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
An abstract, artistic visualization: inside the Heavy Ion Linear Accelerator at Berkeley, boron ions are accelerated through a long tube, gaining tremendous energy, visualized as a bright blue beam of particles. The beam strikes a californium target with a brilliant white flash. From the nuclear collision, a single lawrencium atom briefly exists — depicted as a glowing sphere of deep purple light, the last and heaviest of the actinides. The atom pulses with energy for a few seconds before ejecting alpha particles in a burst of golden sparks, rapidly transforming into lighter elements in a cascade of decay. The purple glow fades and scatters. The final actinide, glimpsed for only moments. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Lawrencium is the last element in the actinide series, and like its neighbors, it has never been produced in visible quantities. Only individual atoms have been created, with the most commonly used isotope lasting less than 3 minutes. There is nothing to photograph or film. The abstract concept of the "final actinide" has some narrative appeal but is not visually compelling enough to recommend generating.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** Estimated at approximately 14.4 g/cm3 (never measured)
- **Reactivity:** Predicted to be easily oxidized by air, steam, and acids; expected to be trivalent like other late actinides
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Estimated melting point of 1627 degrees C (never measured)
- **Appearance:** Completely unknown — predicted to be silvery-white or gray metallic solid
- **Other:** The last element in the actinide series; most commonly studied isotope (Lr-260) has a half-life of only 2.7 minutes; longest-lived isotope (Lr-266) lasts about 11 hours

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Lawrencium is the very last element in the actinide series — the final member of a family of 15 elements that includes uranium and plutonium, like the youngest sibling in a very radioactive family!
2. The first lawrencium atoms were created on Valentine's Day, February 14, 1961, by smashing boron atoms into californium at incredible speeds in a particle accelerator.
3. It is named after Ernest Lawrence, the inventor of the cyclotron — the circular particle accelerator that made it possible to create new elements by smashing atoms together.
