# Iridium (Ir) — Element 77

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** A polished iridium nugget sitting untouched in boiling aqua regia — the acid that dissolves gold — completely unaffected

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small, polished iridium pellet with a brilliant silvery-white luster being dropped into a glass beaker of bubbling, fuming aqua regia — the infamous golden-yellow acid mixture known for dissolving gold and platinum. The acid roils and fumes violently, orange-yellow vapors curling upward, but the iridium sits at the bottom completely untouched, its mirror-bright surface showing not a single pit or bubble. Minutes pass in time-lapse and the acid level drops from evaporation while the iridium pellet remains pristine and gleaming, utterly impervious. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Iridium is the most corrosion-resistant metal known — it resists even aqua regia, the legendary acid mixture that can dissolve gold and platinum. Showing it surviving the "ultimate acid test" creates a compelling narrative of invincibility that kids can instantly grasp. The visual contrast of violent, fuming acid versus a perfectly calm, pristine metal is inherently dramatic.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered

- **Iridium spark plug firing**: Application-focused rather than element-focused

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 22.56 g/cm3 — second densest naturally occurring element after osmium
- **Reactivity:** THE most corrosion-resistant metal known — resists all acids including aqua regia
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** High melting point of 2,446°C; maintains mechanical properties above 1,600°C in air
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal with a slight yellowish cast, very hard and brittle
- **Other:** Named from Greek "iris" meaning "rainbow" because many of its salts are vividly colored

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. A thin layer of iridium found in rocks all over Earth is the key evidence that an asteroid wiped out the dinosaurs 66 million years ago — iridium is rare on Earth but common in space rocks
2. Iridium is the most corrosion-resistant metal ever discovered — not even the strongest acids on Earth can dissolve it
3. Despite being named after the rainbow goddess Iris, the metal itself is silvery-white — it got its name because its chemical compounds come in a dazzling rainbow of colors
