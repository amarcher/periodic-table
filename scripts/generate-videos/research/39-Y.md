# Yttrium (Y) — Element 39

**Should generate video:** Yes

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of fine silvery yttrium metal turnings and shavings piled on a ceramic dish. A spark ignites them and they erupt into a brilliant, blinding white-hot fire — burning with a fierce, almost unbearable intensity. The white flames consume the metal turnings rapidly, glowing like tiny stars. Bright white sparks shoot upward and outward, trailing streaks of light. Wisps of white smoke rise from the pile as the yttrium oxide ash forms. The intense white glow illuminates the entire scene with harsh, pure white light that overwhelms the darkness. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
While bulk yttrium is stable, finely divided yttrium turnings are pyrophoric and ignite spectacularly in air above 400C, burning white-hot. This dramatic combustion creates one of the most visually intense metal fires — a blinding white inferno. The contrast between the calm silvery shavings and the violent white combustion is compelling.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **YAG laser crystal glowing:** Visually interesting but shows a compound, not the pure element
- **Bulk yttrium metal sitting inert:** The silvery metal is stable in air in bulk form, making it visually boring
- **Yttrium oxide powder (used in LED phosphors):** A white powder is not visually dramatic

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 4.47 g/cm3, relatively light for a transition metal
- **Reactivity:** Stable in bulk but pyrophoric as fine turnings; burns white-hot in air above 400C
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1526C, boils at 3336C — very high temperatures
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal with a metallic luster, protected by an oxide film in air
- **Other:** Key ingredient in YAG lasers and white LED phosphors

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Yttrium is one of four elements named after the same tiny Swedish village of Ytterby — the others are terbium, erbium, and ytterbium
2. The white LEDs in your phone screen likely contain yttrium compounds that help convert blue light into white light
3. Yttrium turnings will spontaneously catch fire in air, burning with a blinding white glow — but a solid chunk just sits there calmly
