# Arsenic (As) — Element 33

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Arsenic sublimating — solid gray metallic arsenic heated in a sealed glass tube, converting directly to yellow vapor without melting

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of crystalline gray metallic arsenic inside a sealed glass tube being gently heated from below. The hard, brittle, silver-gray crystals begin to transform directly into a swirling yellowish vapor without ever becoming liquid — sublimating straight from solid to gas. The pale yellow-white fumes rise and swirl inside the glass tube in ghostly, ethereal tendrils. As the vapor contacts the cooler upper portion of the tube, it re-deposits as a thin film of shiny gray crystalline arsenic on the glass walls, creating a dark metallic mirror coating. The cycle of sublimation and deposition creates a dramatic visual of matter shifting between states inside the sealed vessel. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Arsenic's ability to sublimate — skip the liquid phase entirely and go directly from solid to gas — is genuinely unusual and scientifically fascinating. The visual of a gray metallic solid producing ghostly yellow vapor that then re-crystallizes on cooler surfaces is dramatic and educational. It demonstrates an unusual phase transition that most kids have never seen. The sealed-tube approach keeps it safely contained while showcasing this remarkable behavior.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 5.73 g/cm³ — moderately dense
- **Reactivity:** Oxidizes in air; burns with a bluish flame producing white arsenic trioxide fumes with a garlic-like odor
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Sublimes at 614°C at normal atmospheric pressure — skips the liquid phase entirely!
- **Appearance:** Gray (most stable form) is metallic and brittle; also exists as yellow arsenic (molecular, unstable)
- **Other:** Known since ancient times; the word "arsenic" comes from the Greek word for "potent"

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Arsenic can skip being a liquid entirely — when heated, it goes straight from a solid to a gas (called sublimation), like dry ice does, but arsenic does it at a much higher temperature!
2. When arsenic is heated in air, it produces a smell like garlic — this garlic smell was historically used as a test to identify arsenic in minerals.
3. The element's name comes from an ancient Greek word meaning "potent" or "bold" — it's been known since ancient times and has a fascinating (and sometimes dark) history in chemistry.
