## Why This Concept
Rutherfordium's most compelling story is the intense USA vs. USSR naming dispute during the Cold War — the Soviets called it "Kurchatovium" while Americans called it "Rutherfordium." The scientific competition is more visually interesting than the invisible atom itself. However, there is nothing real to film — no visible sample has ever existed.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Aqueous chemistry experiment**: Rf is the heaviest element to have repeated aqueous chemistry experiments, but the experiments involve invisible quantities and specialized detectors — not visually dramatic.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (267Rf) lasts about 48 minutes — relatively long for a superheavy element. Other isotopes last only seconds.
- **How it was made:** First claimed by JINR Dubna (USSR) in 1964 by bombarding plutonium-242 with neon-22. Independently made at UC Berkeley in 1969 by bombarding californium-249 with carbon-12. Credit shared in 1992.
- **Predicted properties:** Behaves like hafnium (its lighter group 4 cousin). Forms tetrachloride RfCl4 and tetrabromide RfBr4 — confirmed experimentally.
- **Other:** First transactinide element. It was the subject of one of the longest naming disputes in chemistry history, lasting from 1964 to 1997.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Rutherfordium was at the center of a "name war" between American and Soviet scientists during the Cold War — the Soviets wanted to call it Kurchatovium (Ku) and the Americans wanted Rutherfordium (Rf). It took over 30 years to settle the argument!
2. It is named after Ernest Rutherford, the physicist who discovered that atoms have a nucleus — basically the person who figured out atoms aren't just tiny solid balls.
3. Even though only a few atoms have ever been made, scientists managed to dissolve them in liquid and study their chemistry — making rutherfordium the heaviest element ever studied in solution more than once.
