## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a silvery ytterbium metal sample in a laboratory press. As pressure is applied, a connected voltmeter display shows the electrical resistance changing dramatically. The metal is compressed and released rhythmically, and the meter needle swings in perfect synchronization — the metal's electrical properties changing with every squeeze. A metal that can feel pressure and report it through electricity. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Ytterbium has notable stress-sensing properties (it changes electrical resistance under pressure, used in stress gauges for explosions and earthquakes). However, this is an invisible property that requires instruments to detect — not visually dramatic at all. The pure metal is yet another generic silvery lanthanide. Ytterbium has a green-yellow divalent ion color, but this is subtle. There is simply nothing visually explosive or dramatic about ytterbium for an 8-second video.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Ytterbium metal appearance:** Indistinguishable from other silvery lanthanides
- **Ytterbium atomic clocks (most precise timekeeping):** Fascinating but invisible physics
- **Ytterbium divalent ion (green-yellow in solution):** Subtle color, not dramatic

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 6.90 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Slowly tarnishes in air; reacts slowly with water; divalent ion is a strong reducing agent
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 824C, boils at 1196C — unusually low boiling point for a lanthanide
- **Appearance:** Soft, silvery metal; divalent compounds are green-yellow
- **Other:** Ytterbium atomic clocks may be even more precise than cesium clocks — potential future redefinition of the second

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Ytterbium is used in stress gauges that measure the shockwaves from nuclear explosions and earthquakes — it can feel and measure the most powerful forces on Earth
2. Ytterbium atomic clocks are so precise they would not lose a single second in 15 billion years — the entire age of the universe
3. Like erbium and terbium, ytterbium is named after the tiny Swedish village of Ytterby — three elements from one village!
