# Fermium (Fm) — Element 100

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** No
**Confidence:** High
**Video concept:** Abstract visualization of fermium atoms being born in the fireball of a thermonuclear explosion

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
An abstract, artistic visualization: deep in the heart of a blinding white thermonuclear fireball, atoms undergo rapid successive neutron captures. A single uranium nucleus absorbs neutron after neutron in a cascade, its atomic number climbing through the actinides in microseconds. The nucleus glows brighter with each capture — shifting from orange to white to an intense electric blue as it reaches element 100. The newborn fermium atom pulses with radiant blue-white energy before beginning its slow radioactive decay, shedding alpha particles that streak away as golden sparks. The entire creation-and-decay cycle plays out in seconds, a fleeting atomic existence born in humanity's most powerful explosion. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Fermium has never been produced in quantities large enough to observe visually as a bulk metal. Pure fermium metal has never been prepared. Its most dramatic story is its discovery — born in the fireball of the first hydrogen bomb. However, this is an abstract visualization rather than a depiction of the real element, which is why the video recommendation is "No."

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Silvery metal prediction rendering:** Would be speculative and dishonest — no one has ever seen bulk fermium metal
- **Fermium in solution showing oxidation states:** Only picogram quantities exist, making solution chemistry nearly impossible to visualize
- **Comparison to other actinides:** Educational but not visually compelling as a standalone video

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** Unknown — never produced in visible quantities as pure metal
- **Reactivity:** Chemistry is typical of late actinides with +3 and accessible +2 oxidation states
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Estimated melting point of 1527 degrees C (never directly measured)
- **Appearance:** Never observed as a bulk metal; predicted to be silvery-gray based on periodic table position
- **Other:** The heaviest element that can be made by neutron bombardment; discovered in hydrogen bomb fallout alongside einsteinium in 1952

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Fermium was secretly discovered in the radioactive debris of the first hydrogen bomb explosion in 1952 — the discovery was classified top secret for years!
2. Fermium is the heaviest element that can be created by simply piling neutrons onto lighter atoms — anything heavier requires smashing atoms together in a particle accelerator.
3. Named after Enrico Fermi, the physicist who built the world's first nuclear reactor under the bleachers of a squash court at the University of Chicago.
