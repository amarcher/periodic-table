## Why This Concept
Uranium glass glowing vivid green under UV light is one of the most visually stunning and eerie demonstrations in chemistry. The fluorescence is caused by uranyl compounds in the glass absorbing UV photons and re-emitting them as visible green light. It's immediately dramatic, otherworldly, and communicates radioactivity in a safe, beautiful way. The glass itself is only very mildly radioactive and is safely collected by hobbyists, making it a perfect "wow" visual without being frightening.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 19.1 g/cm³ — 68% denser than lead; a baseball-sized sphere of uranium would weigh about 9 kg (20 lbs)
- **Reactivity:** Slowly oxidizes in air; pyrophoric when finely divided (powder can spontaneously ignite); weakly radioactive with a half-life of 4.5 billion years
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1,132°C — a dense, silvery metal in solid form
- **Appearance:** Silvery-gray metal that tarnishes to a dull, dark oxide coating when exposed to air
- **Other:** The heaviest naturally occurring element; a single fuel pellet the size of a gummy bear contains as much energy as one ton of coal

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Uranium is so dense that a baseball-sized sphere would weigh about 20 pounds — you could barely lift it with one hand, even though it looks small enough to toss!
2. Uranium glass (glass with a tiny bit of uranium mixed in) glows an eerie, brilliant green under a blacklight — people have been collecting these glowing glasses, plates, and vases since the 1800s, and they're only the tiniest bit radioactive.
3. A single uranium fuel pellet the size of a gummy bear contains as much energy as burning an entire ton of coal — that's why nuclear power plants can generate enormous amounts of electricity from a surprisingly small amount of fuel.
