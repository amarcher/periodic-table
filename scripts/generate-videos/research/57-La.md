# Lanthanum (La) — Element 57

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** Yes
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Lanthanum metal filings igniting spontaneously in air, burning with brilliant white sparks

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of fine silvery-white lanthanum metal filings being sprinkled from above into open air in a dark laboratory. The tiny metal particles ignite spontaneously mid-fall, each one bursting into a brilliant white-hot spark, creating a shower of miniature fireworks cascading downward like a metallic waterfall of light. The sparks trail bright white streaks through the darkness as the pyrophoric metal oxidizes instantly on contact with oxygen. A continuous stream of glittering, self-igniting metal rain. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Finely divided lanthanum is pyrophoric — it ignites spontaneously in air without any external ignition source. This is a dramatic and visually stunning property that creates a shower of white sparks. As the "father" element of the entire lanthanide series (the series is literally named after it), showing its fiery nature is a fitting introduction.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Lanthanum reacting with water:** Slow with cold water, faster with hot but less visually dramatic than spontaneous combustion
- **Lanthanum tarnishing in air:** Too slow and subtle for 8 seconds
- **Lanthanum in lighter flints:** Better attributed to cerium specifically

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 6.15 g/cm3 — noticeably dense
- **Reactivity:** Tarnishes rapidly in air; powder is pyrophoric (self-igniting); reacts slowly with cold water
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 920C, boils at 3464C
- **Appearance:** Soft silvery-white metal, easily cut with a knife, tarnishes to dark grey
- **Other:** The entire "lanthanide" series of 15 elements is named after lanthanum

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. The entire lanthanide series — 15 elements! — is named after lanthanum, making it the "parent" of the rare earths
2. Lanthanum powder is pyrophoric, meaning it catches fire all by itself just from touching air
3. Its name comes from the Greek "lanthanein" meaning "to lie hidden" because it was so hard to find and separate from other elements
