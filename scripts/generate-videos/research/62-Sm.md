# Samarium (Sm) — Element 62

## Video Recommendation
**Should generate video:** No
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Samarium-cobalt magnet resisting demagnetization at extreme temperatures (red-hot and still magnetic)

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small silvery samarium-cobalt magnet sitting on a heat-resistant surface in a dark laboratory. A torch flame heats the magnet until it glows dull red-hot — yet iron filings sprinkled nearby are still visibly pulled toward it, clinging to its surface even at extreme temperatures. The magnet continues to attract metal particles while glowing with heat, demonstrating its extraordinary thermal stability. Other ordinary magnets would have lost all magnetism long ago at this temperature. A magnet so stubborn it refuses to quit even when red hot. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
While samarium-cobalt magnets are genuinely impressive (maintaining magnetism up to 550C), the visual is somewhat subtle — a heated magnet still attracting things. It lacks the explosive drama of cesium hitting water or neodymium magnets crushing objects. The pure metal itself is an unremarkable silvery-white, and samarium's most notable applications are in alloys rather than as a pure element.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Samarium metal oxidizing in air:** Generic silvery lanthanide tarnishing — not distinctive
- **Samarium in cancer treatment (Sm-153):** Medical application, not visually dramatic as pure element
- **Samarium igniting (pyrophoric powder):** Similar to other lanthanide powder ignitions

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 7.52 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Ignites in air at 150C; tarnishes in air
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1072C, boils at 1794C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal, relatively hard for a lanthanide
- **Other:** Samarium-cobalt magnets work at temperatures up to 550C where neodymium magnets fail

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Samarium-cobalt magnets can work in extreme heat up to 550C — they are used in jet engines and spacecraft where it is too hot for any other magnet
2. Samarium is named after a Russian mining engineer, Colonel Samarsky — the first element ever named after a person
3. A radioactive form of samarium (Sm-153) is used to treat bone cancer by targeting cancer cells directly
