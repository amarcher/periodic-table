## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small pile of dark amorphous boron powder on a ceramic dish. A flame touches the powder and it ignites, erupting into a spectacular brilliant green fire. The vivid emerald-green flames dance and flicker intensely, casting bright green light across the scene. Sparks of glowing green shoot upward from the burning pile. The flames shift between deep forest green and bright electric green as different portions of the powder combust. Wisps of white smoke curl upward through the green glow. ## Why This Concept
Boron burns with one of the most distinctive and vivid flame colors of any element — a brilliant, saturated green that is immediately striking and rare in nature. Green flames are uncommon and visually captivating, especially for kids. This is the same property that makes boron compounds essential in green fireworks, connecting the science to something kids already love.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Boron crystal structure**: Crystalline boron is dark and not visually interesting on its own
- **Boric acid flame test**: Similar green but less dramatic than burning pure boron powder
- **Boron in rocket ignition**: Too industrial and explosive for a kids' app context

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** Moderately dense (2.34 g/cm3); hard enough to scratch most metals
- **Reactivity:** Burns in air with a characteristic green flame; relatively inert at room temperature
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Extremely high melting point (2076C) — one of the highest of any metalloid
- **Appearance:** Pure amorphous boron is a dark brown-to-black powder; crystalline boron is dark, lustrous, and very hard
- **Other:** Second hardest element after carbon (diamond); used in bulletproof vests (boron carbide) and green fireworks

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Boron is the secret ingredient that makes green fireworks green — it burns with a brilliant emerald-colored flame
2. Boron carbide is so hard it is used in tank armor and bulletproof vests — it is the third hardest material known after diamond and cubic boron nitride
3. Borax, a common household cleaner, is a boron compound — the element boron was first isolated from borax in 1808
