## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a red-hot ceramic crucible tilting slowly, pouring a stream of molten aluminum — a shimmering silvery liquid glowing faintly orange from the heat. The molten metal cascades in a smooth, mirror-like stream into a graphite mold below, splashing slightly on impact and sending tiny bright sparks upward. The surface of the liquid metal ripples and reflects light like mercury, with a faint orange-red glow from the 660-degree heat. Wisps of heat shimmer rise from the surface as the metal begins to solidify at the edges, transitioning from glowing liquid to solid silver. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Molten aluminum has a mesmerizing mirror-like liquid surface that looks almost alien — silvery and reflective yet flowing like water. The pour creates a satisfying visual arc, and the transition from glowing liquid to solid silver is captivating. This matches the successful gold video concept (molten pour) while having its own distinct silvery character.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Thermite reaction (aluminum + iron oxide)**: Extremely dramatic but focuses on a compound reaction rather than pure aluminum behavior
- **Aluminum foil in hydrochloric acid**: Produces hydrogen bubbles but is visually mundane — just fizzing foil
- **Aluminum oxide formation (anodizing)**: Invisible chemical change, not visually compelling

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** Very light for a metal at 2.70 g/cm3 — about one-third the weight of steel
- **Reactivity:** Forms a thin, tough oxide layer instantly when exposed to air that protects it from further corrosion
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 660 C, boils at 2,519 C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white, soft, lightweight metal with a characteristic bright metallic luster
- **Other:** Most abundant metal in Earth's crust (about 8% by weight), yet it was once more valuable than gold because it was so hard to extract

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. In the 1800s, aluminum was rarer than gold — Napoleon III served his most important guests with aluminum plates while everyone else got gold ones
2. A single aluminum can that you recycle saves enough electricity to power a TV for three hours
3. The tip of the Washington Monument is capped with a small pyramid of aluminum — when it was placed there in 1884, aluminum was a precious metal
