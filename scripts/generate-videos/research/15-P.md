## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)

The phosphorus emits an ethereal, ghostly green-blue glow as it slowly oxidizes in air — faint wisps of white smoke curl upward from its surface. Suddenly the phosphorus self-ignites, erupting into a brilliant intense white-orange flame with dense billowing clouds of white phosphorus pentoxide smoke swirling dramatically upward. ## Why This Concept
Phosphorus is literally the element that gave us the word "phosphorescence" (even though its glow is actually chemiluminescence). The eerie green glow in darkness followed by spontaneous ignition is one of the most dramatic two-act sequences in chemistry — mysterious beauty followed by violent energy. The green glow is unique among elements and deeply captivating.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Red phosphorus striking a match head**: Familiar but the phosphorus itself is not the visual star — the match flame is
- **Phosphorus burning in pure oxygen in a jar**: More intense but loses the haunting green glow phase which is the most unique part
- **Phosphorus under water (stored for safety)**: Visually uninteresting — just a waxy lump in water

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** White phosphorus: 1.82 g/cm3; Red phosphorus: 2.27 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** White phosphorus is pyrophoric — it spontaneously catches fire in air above about 30 C
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** White phosphorus melts at just 44.1 C (warm to the touch!) and boils at 280.5 C
- **Appearance:** White phosphorus is a translucent waxy solid that glows green in the dark; red phosphorus is a dark reddish-brown powder
- **Other:** The element's name comes from the Greek "phosphoros" meaning "light-bearer" — it was discovered in 1669 by an alchemist boiling urine

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Phosphorus was discovered by a German alchemist named Hennig Brand who was boiling huge amounts of his own urine trying to make gold — instead he found a glowing element
2. White phosphorus glows green in the dark all by itself because it is slowly reacting with oxygen in the air — it is so reactive it has to be stored underwater
3. Your DNA, your bones, and your teeth all contain phosphorus — every living thing on Earth needs it to survive
