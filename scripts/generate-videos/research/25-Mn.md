## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of dark purplish-black crystals of potassium permanganate being dropped into a glass beaker of perfectly clear water. The instant the crystals touch the water, intense deep purple-violet tendrils begin streaming downward like ink, swirling and curling in mesmerizing fluid dynamics. The saturated purple color is so vivid it seems to glow. The tendrils spiral and bloom outward, creating fractal-like patterns as they diffuse through the water. More crystals dissolve, intensifying the rich violet color until the entire solution becomes a deep, jewel-like purple. The purple streams catch the light, creating an otherworldly, hypnotic display. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
While manganese metal itself is visually unremarkable (gray-white, brittle), its most famous compound — potassium permanganate — creates one of the most visually stunning dissolution sequences in all of chemistry. The deep purple color is so intense that it remains visible even when diluted 175,000 times. The swirling purple tendrils in water are mesmerizing and immediately capture attention. This showcases manganese's ability to exist in the +7 oxidation state, which produces this extraordinary color.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 7.21 g/cm³ — similar density to iron
- **Reactivity:** Not particularly reactive in bulk form; burns when finely divided
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1,246°C
- **Appearance:** Gray-white, hard, brittle metal that tarnishes in air
- **Other:** Essential for steelmaking — nearly all steel contains manganese; also critical for photosynthesis in plants

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Potassium permanganate (a manganese compound) is so intensely purple that you can still see the color even when it's diluted 175,000 times — that's like one drop in a swimming pool!
2. Every piece of steel ever made contains manganese — without it, steel would be brittle and useless.
3. Plants use manganese to split water molecules during photosynthesis, which is how they make the oxygen we breathe!
