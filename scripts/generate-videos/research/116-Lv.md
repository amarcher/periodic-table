## Why This Concept
Livermorium is one of the shortest-lived superheavy elements, lasting only about 80 milliseconds in its most stable form. It has no experimentally confirmed chemical properties and nothing visually distinctive. Its story is primarily about the collaboration between the Russian JINR and American Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. There is genuinely nothing to show in a video.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (293Lv) lasts about 80 milliseconds. That is faster than a human blink (~300ms).
- **How it was made:** First synthesized on July 19, 2000, at JINR Dubna, Russia. They bombarded curium-248 with calcium-48 ions. It was a collaboration between JINR and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. Named by IUPAC on May 30, 2012.
- **Predicted properties:** Expected to be in group 16, below polonium. May exhibit some properties of a volatile metal or metalloid. Relativistic effects are significant but not as dramatic as for oganesson.
- **Other:** Named after the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in Livermore, California, which has been involved in the discovery of several superheavy elements through its collaboration with JINR.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Livermorium lasts about 80 milliseconds — that is less time than it takes you to blink your eyes (a blink takes about 300 milliseconds). It is gone before you could even start to look at it.
2. It is named after Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in California, which is one of the most famous science labs in America. The lab itself is named after the city of Livermore, which is named after a rancher named Robert Livermore.
3. Only about 6 isotopes of livermorium have ever been detected, with only a handful of individual atoms observed for each. If you added up every atom of livermorium ever created, the total would not even be visible under the most powerful microscope.
