## Why This Concept
Tennessine's most interesting story is the international collaboration required to create it. Berkelium-249 (the target material) is incredibly rare and can only be produced at Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee. It had to be shipped to Dubna, Russia, where it was bombarded with calcium ions for 70 days at 7 trillion ions per second. This global scientific relay is compelling as a story, but the element itself is invisible and ephemeral.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Half-life:** Most stable isotope (294Ts) lasts about 80 milliseconds.
- **How it was made:** Discovered in 2010 by a joint team at JINR Dubna, Lawrence Livermore, and Oak Ridge National Laboratory. They bombarded berkelium-249 with calcium-48 ions for 70 days at 7 trillion ions per second. The berkelium target itself took over a year to produce at Oak Ridge. Officially named in November 2016.
- **Predicted properties:** Classified as a halogen (group 17, like fluorine and chlorine), but predicted to behave very differently from lighter halogens due to relativistic effects. May be a metalloid or even a metal rather than a reactive nonmetal.
- **Other:** Named after Tennessee, home to Oak Ridge National Laboratory, Vanderbilt University, and the University of Tennessee — all key contributors to the discovery. It has the second-highest atomic number of any known element.

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Tennessine is technically a halogen — in the same family as the gas in your toothpaste (fluorine) and the liquid used to clean swimming pools (chlorine). But scientists predict it would behave nothing like them — it might even be a metal!
2. Creating tennessine required a global relay: first, scientists in Tennessee spent over a year making a tiny amount of berkelium, then shipped it to Russia, where another team bombarded it with calcium atoms for 70 days, firing 7 TRILLION calcium ions per second.
3. Tennessine is one of four elements (along with nihonium, moscovium, and oganesson) that were all officially named on the same day in November 2016 — the biggest single-day addition to the periodic table in modern history.
