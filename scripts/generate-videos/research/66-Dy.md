# Dysprosium (Dy) — Element 66

**Should generate video:** No
**Confidence:** Medium
**Video concept:** Dysprosium's magnetostrictive shape change in an alloy (Terfenol-D) under a magnetic field

## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a small rod of Terfenol-D (terbium-dysprosium-iron alloy) clamped in a fixture in a dark laboratory. An electromagnet is switched on, and the rod visibly elongates — stretching by a tiny but measurable amount as the magnetic field forces the atomic lattice to realign. A dial indicator pressed against the end of the rod shows the needle jumping as the rod grows and shrinks with each pulse of the magnetic field. The rod pulses rhythmically, growing and shrinking like a tiny metallic heartbeat driven by invisible magnetic force. A metal that breathes when magnetism commands it. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Dysprosium is a key ingredient in Terfenol-D, the most magnetostrictive material known — it physically changes shape in a magnetic field. However, the shape change is only about 2000 microstrains (0.2%), which is essentially invisible to the naked eye without magnification or measurement instruments. This makes it hard to create a visually dramatic 8-second video. The pure metal itself is a generic silvery lanthanide with no distinctive visual features.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Dysprosium metal appearance:** Identical to other silvery lanthanides — not distinctive
- **Dysprosium in nuclear control rods:** Invisible neutron absorption, impossible to film
- **Dysprosium added to neodymium magnets for heat resistance:** Improvement is invisible

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 8.55 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Slowly tarnishes in air; reacts slowly with cold water, rapidly with hot
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1412C, boils at 2567C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metallic with bright luster
- **Other:** Key ingredient in the most magnetostrictive material known (Terfenol-D) — alloy physically changes shape in magnetic fields

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Dysprosium is used in an alloy that literally changes shape when you put it in a magnetic field — a real-life shape-shifting metal!
2. Its name comes from the Greek "dysprositos" meaning "hard to get" because it was extremely difficult for chemists to isolate
3. Dysprosium is added to neodymium magnets to make them work in the extreme heat of electric car motors and wind turbines
