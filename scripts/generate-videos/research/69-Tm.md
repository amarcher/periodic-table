## Video Prompt (for Veo 3.1 Fast)
Extreme close-up of a thulium-doped material in a dark room. A UV lamp switches on and the sample emits a soft, bright blue luminescence — a cool, ethereal blue glow that contrasts with the warm reds and greens of europium and terbium. A euro banknote is brought under the UV light and thulium security features glow blue alongside the red europium and green terbium, creating a trio of lanthanide colors. The blue glow is gentle but pure, a distinctive ice-blue hue. Dark background, cinematic lighting, extreme close-up, no text, no watermarks, no logos.

## Why This Concept
Thulium ions produce a blue luminescence under UV excitation, which is used in anti-counterfeiting features in euro banknotes. However, the blue glow is less intense and dramatic than europium's red or terbium's green, making it the weakest of the three "euro banknote" lanthanides visually. The pure metal is a generic silvery lanthanide. Thulium is the second rarest lanthanide (after promethium), but rarity is not visible. Overall not compelling enough for a standalone video.

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 9.32 g/cm3
- **Reactivity:** Slowly tarnishes in air; reacts slowly with water
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Melts at 1545C, boils at 1950C
- **Appearance:** Silvery-white metal with bright luster; compounds emit blue luminescence under UV
- **Other:** Second rarest naturally occurring lanthanide; used in portable X-ray machines and surgical lasers

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Thulium is used in portable X-ray machines that do not need electricity — the radioactive isotope Tm-170 generates X-rays all by itself
2. Thulium makes the blue glow in euro banknotes under UV light, working alongside europium (red) and terbium (green) as anti-counterfeiting security
3. Thulium is named after "Thule," the ancient name for Scandinavia — a land at the edge of the known world, fitting for one of the rarest elements on Earth
