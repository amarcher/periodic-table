## Why This Concept
Neon's iconic red-orange glow in a discharge tube is the single most recognizable visual associated with this element — it's what gave "neon signs" their name. The vivid, saturated light pulsing through a glass tube is mesmerizing and immediately communicates what makes neon special. Since neon is an invisible, colorless, odorless gas under normal conditions, the discharge tube is the only way to make it visually dramatic.

## Alternative Video Concepts Considered
- **Neon sign spelling out a word:** Too commercial and distracting — the focus should be on the element itself, not signage
- **Liquid neon in a cryogenic container:** Neon liquefies at -246°C; visually it would just look like a clear liquid — not distinctive
- **Neon balloon floating upward:** Neon is lighter than air so a neon balloon would float, but it would look identical to a helium balloon — boring and indistinguishable

## Interesting Properties for App Display
- **Density:** 0.90 g/L — about two-thirds the density of air, so a neon-filled balloon would float
- **Reactivity:** Completely inert — neon forms no stable chemical compounds at all, making it one of the least reactive elements in existence
- **Melting/Boiling Point:** Boils at -246°C; has the narrowest liquid range of any element (just 2.5°C between melting and boiling)
- **Appearance:** Colorless, odorless, invisible gas; glows brilliant red-orange when electricity passes through it
- **Other:** Fifth most abundant element in the universe, but extremely rare on Earth (only 0.0018% of the atmosphere)

## Kid-Friendly Factoids
1. Neon signs aren't always actually neon — only the red-orange ones are! Other colors use different gases like argon (purple) or mercury vapor (blue), but we call them all "neon signs" because neon was the first gas used.
2. The name "neon" comes from the Greek word "neos" meaning "new" — its discoverers were so excited to find a new element hiding in the air that they named it after the thrill of discovery.
3. Liquid neon is an incredible refrigerant — it has over 40 times more cooling power per unit volume than liquid helium, making it one of the most efficient coolants known to science.
